Exploring Data: Transverse Momentum¶

The first feature is pT which is a label for transverse momentum which has units GeV. Specifically, the transverse momentum is the component of momentum which is perpendicular to the beamline. This can be calculated by taking the square root of the square of the x and y component of momentum on the xy-plane. So, transverse momentum on the xy-plane is given by the equation: $p_T = \sqrt{p_x^2+p_y^2}$.

According to the lab manual, a Higgs boson is produced at large transverse momentum. Below, we have a histogram and semi-log histogram of the Higgs data and the QCD background. The x-axis is the transverse momentum in GeV and the y-axis is the asssociated number of events (counts). We notice how there is a notable spike when the transverse momentum is a value of approximately 480 GeV. From this, we can infer a Higgs boson is produced when the transverse momentum is a value of approximately 480 GeV.

Exploring Data: Pseudo-Rapidity¶

The second feature is $\eta$ (eta) which is a label for pseudo-rapidity. Specifically, pseudo-rapidity is a commonly used spatial coordinate in experimental particle physics. It describes the angle of a particle relative to the beam axis. Pseudo-rapidity is given by the equation $\eta = -\ln[\tan(\frac{\theta}{2})]$, where $\theta$ represents the angle between the particle three-momentum $p$ and the positive direction of the beam axis. Another equation which describes pseudo-rapdiity as a function of three-momentum $p$ is: $\eta = arctanh(\frac{p_L}{|p|})$, where $p_L$ is the component of momentum parallel to the beam axis or longitudinal momentum. According to the lab manual, $\eta$ is a geometric quantity which is a function of polar angle $\theta$ that goes from $\inf$ to $-\inf$ as $\theta$ goes from 0 to $\pi$.

Here, we have a histogram which resembles a Gaussian distribution with a mean of zero. In particle physics, an angle of zero is generally along the beam axis. Thus, particles with large pseudo-rapidity values are lost which means we can reduce the background data by omitting data with a signal greater than $2\sigma$.

Exploring Data: Azimuthal Angle¶

The third feature is $\phi$ (phi) which is a label for the azimuthal angle. Specifically, the azimuthal angle is the angle between a plane perpendicular to the beam and a reference vector. The azimuthal angle is given by the equation: $\phi = \arccos(\frac{x}{r})$, where x is a plane perpendicular to the beam and r is a reference vector. According to the lab manual, the azimuthal angle goes around the beam.

Here, we cannot tell if there is any signficance of the azimuthal angle between the Higgs and QCD data. Specifically, there doesn't seem to be any distinguishable patterns or correlations in which we can comment on for the azimuthal angle between the Higgs and QCD data. At least, if we are talking about the counts of an azimuthal angle. Perhaps, there is some sort of valuable data in which we can observe if we were to weigh the azimuthal angle against other feature in the data set.

Exploring Data: Mass¶

The fourth feature is mass which has units GeV. Simply, this feature describes the mass of a particle. The mass is given by the equation $E^2 = p^2 + m^2$, where $E$ and $p$ are energy and momentum respectively.

From the histrogram plots, we notice a spike at approximately 125 GeV. According to Wikipedia, the Higgs boson has a mass of $125.18 ± 0.16 \frac{GeV}{c^2}$. This explains why we see a lot of counts around 125 GeV which is approximately the mass of the Higgs boson. Thus, our data is consistent with what we expect in reality.

Exploring Data: Angularity¶

The eighth feature is angularity. Specifically, angularity describes a separator between QCD and heavy-object jets. From Alex Schruy's repository called qsvm_jet_tagging, the angularity is given by the equation: $\theta = \frac{1}{m_J} \sum_{i \in j} E_i \sin^{-2}(\theta)(1 - \cos^{3}(\theta))$, where $m_J$ is the mass of the jet.

Here, we notice a peak of approximately 0 in both the Higgs data and the QCD background in the histograms below. Specifically, the Higgs data has a peak of approximately 3000 counts while the QCD background has a peak of approximately 7000 counts. So we can infer that the angularity is much larger for the Higgs boson which is shown from the histogram below since the angularity at 0 is less for the Higgs data compared to the QCD background given our definition of angularity as a separator between QCD and heavy-object jets.

Exploring Data: $\tau_1$¶

The ninth feature is t1 which is a label for $\tau_1$. Specifically $\tau_1$ is the 1-subjettiness. $\tau_1$ is given by the equation: $\tau_n = \frac{1}{d_0} \sum_{k=1}(p_{T,k}\min{\Delta R_{1,k}, \Delta R_{2,k}, ..., \Delta R_{N,k}})$ with $n=1$. Here, $d_0$ is the normalization factor given by the equation $d_0 = \sum_k p_{T,k}R_0$, where $R_0$ is the characteristic jet radius used in the original jet clustering algorithm. So, we can see that $\tau_1$ describes a jet composed of 1 subjet. We found the equation given by equation (2.2) in this document: https://arxiv.org/pdf/1011.2268.pdf.

Here, we notice that there is a peak at approximately 0.7 in the Higgs data and a peak at approximately 0.1 in the QCD background. However, we have much more counts at the peak of the QCD background compared to the peak of the Higgs data. Specifically, the QCD background has a peak of approximately 2400 counts while the Higgs data has a peak of approximately 2000 counts. We notice that the background drowns the Higgs data so it would be very hard to differentiate the Higgs data from the background.

Exploring Data: $k_t \Delta R$¶

The fourteenth feature is ktDeltaR which is a label for $k_t \Delta R$. Specifically, $k_t \Delta R$ is the jet substructure moment. $k_t \Delta R$ is given by the equation: $\Delta R = \sqrt{\Delta \eta^2 + \Delta \phi^2}$. According the the lab manual, R is the cone size of a jet. So, $k_t \Delta R$ can be described as a cluster sequence where $\Delta R$ of two subjets within a large-R jet.

Below, we have histograms of the counts of $k_t \Delta R$. We notice there are spikes at approximately 0 and 0.5 for the Higgs data and spikes at approximately 0 for the QCD background. So, there is a spike at approximately 0 for the Higgs data but it would be very difficult to detect considering there is very large background noise. However, it would be easier to detect the spike at approximately 0.5 since there is less background noise. We assume these spikes are signficant because they tell us that the difference of the radius of the subjets have to be a certain value.
